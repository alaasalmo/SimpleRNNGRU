# Introduction

The rapid growth of digital news platforms, financial websites, and social media has generated a massive amount of textual information related to financial markets. 


Investors, analysts, and financial institutions increasingly rely on this information to understand market trends and support decision-making processes. 
As a result, sentiment analysis has become an important research area in the financial domain 
because it can automatically determine whether a piece of text expresses positive, negative, or neutral sentiment.

Sentiment analysis is a branch of Natural Language Processing (NLP) that focuses on extracting opinions, attitudes,
 emotions, and feelings from textual data. In the context of financial news, sentiment analysis can be used to evaluate 
 market perception and identify patterns that may influence stock prices and investment decisions. 
 The increasing volume of financial news has created a need for automated techniques that can efficiently analyze large amounts of 
 textual information.

Recent advances in artificial intelligence, particularly deep learning, have significantly improved the performance of sentiment analysis systems. Deep learning models such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs) are capable of learning complex patterns and contextual relationships within text data. These models have demonstrated promising results in text classification tasks, including financial sentiment analysis.

Despite their effectiveness, deep learning models often require substantial computational resources and long training times, especially when processing large datasets. Traditional approaches typically depend on powerful and expensive servers equipped with high-performance hardware. This creates challenges related to infrastructure costs, scalability, and resource utilization.







To increate the volume of analysis of the financial news to understand the market trends and support the decision makers to take the right path, we will need use new methods with deeplearning and also required to build these models of deeplearnig and traing them.
Training the models required expensive servers or machines (CPUs/GPU's) and memorys, our idea is to implement the training on distributed system.  

To address these challenges, distributed computing technologies can be employed to distribute training workloads 
across multiple computing resources. Containerization technologies such as Docker provide a 
consistent and portable execution environment, while Kubernetes offers automated deployment, workload scheduling, resource management, and scalability for distributed applications. These technologies make it possible to train deep learning models efficiently without relying on a single high cost server.

The objective of this project is to develop and improve two deep learning models, SimpleRNN and GRU, for financial news sentiment analysis. Model enhancement techniques, 
including Bidirectional architectures, class balancing methods, and text augmentation approaches, are applied to improve classification performance. The enhanced models are then containerized using Docker 
and deployed within a Kubernetes environment to support distributed training and execution. 
Finally, the project evaluates both the sentiment classification performance of the models and the system-level performance 
of the distributed training infrastructure.

The project is organized into three phases. The first phase focuses on developing and improving the sentiment analysis models. In first phase, we will try to compare between these two models before and after the improvement. 
The second phase involves containerizing the models using Docker to create reproducible deployment environments. The third phase deploys the containerized models on a Kubernetes cluster to enable distributed execution, resource management, and performance evaluation.
In the third phase, we will try to compare between the result of phase one (the run of sinlge machine) and the run with Kubenrnetes. We will build the Kubernetes on one server to simulate the process for many pods.

The researcher investigates if text data augmentation techniques can improve sentiment analysis performance when datasets are small, imbalanced, or have limited labelled samples.

In this article, they compare four types of augmentation methods: 1-Easy Data Augmentation (EDA), 2-Back Translation (BT), 3-BART, and 4-PREDATOR—across seven sentiment analysis datasets and seven classifiers, including LSTM, GRU, CNN, BERT, ERNIE, SVM, and random forest.

The results show that data augmentation generally improves classification performance, especially for small and imbalanced datasets, with back translation and PREDATOR often producing the best results.

The article provides several important statistics showing how text data augmentation improves sentiment analysis performance. The researchers evaluated 7 classifiers (LSTM, GRU, CNN, BERT, ERNIE, SVM, and Random Forest) on 7 sentiment-analysis datasets, including the Financial PhraseBank dataset (2,264 samples), IEMOCAP (9,292 samples), SEMAINE (5,627 samples), and SST-2 (9,613 samples).

The strongest result was achieved when the PREDATOR augmentation method improved LSTM performance by 67% on the IEMOCAP dataset, while improvements of 60% on Financial PhraseBank and 10% on SEMAINE were also reported.

The objective of this article is studying the effectiveness of text data augmentation methods for sentiment analysis. Compare different augmentation techniques under challenging conditions such as imbalanced datasets and small datasets, and analyse how different classifiers (including GRU and LSTM) respond to augmented data.
