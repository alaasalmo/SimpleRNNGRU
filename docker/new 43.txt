## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 Sentiment Analysis

Sentiment analysis is a subfield of Natural Language Processing (NLP) that focuses on identifying opinions, attitudes, emotions, and sentiments expressed in textual data. With the rapid growth of digital information from news websites, social media platforms, online reviews, and discussion forums, sentiment analysis has become an important tool for extracting meaningful insights from large volumes of unstructured text. Researchers have applied sentiment analysis in various domains including e-commerce, healthcare, marketing, finance, and social media monitoring.

In the financial sector, sentiment analysis has gained considerable attention because financial news and public opinion can influence investor behavior and stock market movements. Agarwal investigated sentiment analysis of financial news headlines and demonstrated that market sentiment extracted from news articles can provide valuable insight into stock price trends. The study highlighted that news is one of the most influential sources of information for investors and can significantly impact market sentiment.

### 2.2 Deep Learning for NLP

Traditional machine learning methods such as Naïve Bayes, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) have been widely used for text classification tasks. However, these approaches often require extensive feature engineering and may struggle to capture contextual relationships between words.

Recent advances in deep learning have significantly improved NLP performance. Deep neural networks can automatically learn complex semantic representations from textual data without manual feature extraction. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs) have become popular architectures for sentiment analysis because they are capable of learning sequential dependencies in text.

### 2.3 Simple Recurrent Neural Networks (SimpleRNN)

Recurrent Neural Networks are designed to process sequential data by maintaining hidden states that capture information from previous time steps. Unlike traditional feedforward neural networks, RNNs can utilize contextual information from earlier words in a sentence, making them suitable for NLP applications.

Despite their effectiveness, traditional RNNs suffer from the vanishing gradient problem when processing long sequences. During training, important information from earlier time steps may gradually disappear, limiting the model's ability to learn long-term dependencies. This limitation has motivated researchers to develop improved recurrent architectures such as LSTM and GRU.

### 2.4 Gated Recurrent Units (GRU)

The Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) was introduced as an improved recurrent architecture designed to overcome the vanishing gradient problem. GRUs use two gating mechanisms, namely the update gate and reset gate, to control the flow of information through the network.

Compared with LSTM, GRUs contain fewer parameters because they do not require a separate memory cell and output gate. As a result, GRUs generally require less computational overhead while maintaining competitive predictive performance. Santur implemented a GRU-based sentiment analysis model using more than 243,000 customer reviews and reported an accuracy of approximately 95%, demonstrating the effectiveness of GRU networks for sentiment classification.

### 2.5 Bidirectional RNN and Bidirectional GRU

Bidirectional recurrent architectures extend traditional recurrent networks by processing text sequences in both forward and backward directions. This allows the model to capture information from both preceding and subsequent words within a sentence, leading to a richer contextual representation.

Xu et al. proposed a Bidirectional GRU model for text sentiment classification and achieved an accuracy of 94.8% on the test dataset. Their results demonstrated that bidirectional architectures can improve classification performance by leveraging context from both directions of a sequence. These findings suggest that Bidirectional GRU models may provide superior performance compared with traditional unidirectional recurrent networks.

### 2.6 Data Augmentation

Data augmentation has become a widely used technique for improving model generalization and addressing class imbalance problems. In NLP applications, augmentation methods include synonym replacement, random insertion, random deletion, random swapping, and back translation.

The primary objective of text augmentation is to increase dataset diversity without collecting additional labeled data. While augmentation can improve model robustness, excessive modification of text may introduce noise and alter the original semantic meaning of sentences. Therefore, the effectiveness of augmentation often depends on dataset characteristics and the quality of generated samples.

### 2.7 Distributed Training using Kubernetes

Training deep learning models often requires substantial computational resources, especially when processing large-scale datasets. Distributed machine learning enables training workloads to be shared across multiple computing nodes, reducing training time and improving resource utilization.

Container technologies such as Docker provide a consistent environment for deploying machine learning applications, while Kubernetes offers automated orchestration, scaling, and resource management. TensorFlow's MultiWorkerMirroredStrategy enables distributed training by synchronizing model updates across multiple workers. The integration of Docker and Kubernetes has emerged as an effective approach for deploying scalable machine learning systems in cloud environments.

### 2.8 Research Gap

Previous studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of sentiment analysis using RNN, LSTM, GRU, and Bidirectional GRU architectures. However, most studies focus primarily on model accuracy and are executed on standalone computing environments. Limited research has investigated the deployment of financial sentiment analysis models within distributed Kubernetes environments while simultaneously evaluating baseline models, bidirectional architectures, class balancing techniques, and text augmentation strategies. This project aims to address this gap by comparing SimpleRNN and GRU models, evaluating several model improvement techniques, and deploying the training workflow using Docker and Kubernetes-based distributed TensorFlow infrastructure.
